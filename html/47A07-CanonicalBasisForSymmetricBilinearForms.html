canonical basis for symmetric bilinear forms


If B:V×V→K is a symmetric bilinear form over a finite-dimensional vector space, where the characteristic of the field is not 2, then we may prove that there is an orthogonal basis such that B is represented by

Recall that a bilinear form has a well-defined rank, and denote this by r.

If K=ℝ we may choose a basis such that a1=⋯=at=1, at+1=⋯=at+p=-1 and at+p+j=0, for some integers p and t, where 1≤j≤n-t-p. Furthermore, these integers are invariants of the bilinear form. This is known as Sylvester’s Law of Inertia. B is positive definite if and only if t=n, p=0. Such a form constitutes a real inner product space.

If K=ℂ we may go further and choose a basis such that a1=⋯=ar=1 and ar+j=0, where 1≤j≤n-r.

If K=Fp we may choose a basis such that a1=⋯=ar-1=1,

ar=n or ar=1; and ar+j=0, where 1≤j≤n-r, and n is the least positive quadratic non-residue.
